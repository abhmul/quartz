Minimizing Binary Cross-Entropy

Last updated April 25, 2022

    Table of Contents

    Dates: 2022-02-17, 2022-02-20, 2022-02-21

    Consider the following setup:

    1. Probability Space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$
    2. Random Vector $U: \Omega \to \mathbb{F}{2}^{n}$ for $n \in \mathbb{N}$. This represents the input sequence. For shorthand $U{i}=\pi_{i}\circ U$, where $\pi_i$ is the $i$th projection map for $i \in [n]$.
    3. Measureable Functions
      1. Encoder $f: \mathbb{F}^{n}_{2} \to M$ where $(M, d)$ is a metric space.
      2. Noise Function $g: M \times \Omega \to M$
      3. Decoder $h: M \to \mathbb{F}^{n}_{2}$
    4. Denote
      1. $F = f \circ U$
      2. $G(\omega) = g(F(\omega), \omega)$
      3. $H = h \circ G$

    Definition 1: The Bit Error Rate of $(f,g,h)$ is $$ \begin{align*} \mathbb{B}(f,g,h) &= \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{n} \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}1[U_{i}\neq H_{i}]\right]\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}[1[U_{i}\neq H_{i}]]\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{P}(U_{i}\neq H_{i}) \end{align*} $$ Theorem 2: The decoder function $$h_{i}(y)= \begin{cases} 1 & \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G=y)> \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G=y) \\0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$ is the unique (up to modifications on $G_{}(\mathbb{P})$-null sets and measureable subsets of $E = {y: \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G=y) = \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G=y)}$) decoder function that minimizes bit error rate. It is known as the Bayes Classifier. $G_{}(\mathbb{P})$ is the (pushforward) probability measure on $M$ induced by $G$.

    Proof: Let $h$ be some decoder function. We decompose the expression for bit error rate: $$\begin{align*} \mathbb{B}(f,g,h) &= \frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{P}(U_{i}\neq H_{i})\\&= \frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\int_{\Omega}\mathbb{P}(U_{i}\neq h_{i}(G)|G)d \mathbb{P}(\omega)\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\int_{\Omega}\mathbb{E}(1[U_{i}=1]1[h_{i}(G)=0] + 1[U_{i}=0]1[h_{i}(G)=1]|G)d \mathbb{P}(\omega)\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\int_{\Omega}1[h_{i}(G)=0]\mathbb{E}(1[U_{i}=1]|G) + 1[h_{i}(G)=1]\mathbb{E}(1[U_{i}=0]|G)d \mathbb{P}(\omega)\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\int_{h_i(G)=0}\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G)d \mathbb{P}(\omega) + \int_{h_{i}(G)=1}\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G)d \mathbb{P}(\omega) \end{align*}$$

    Observe that when we let $h_{i}$ be defined as in the statement of the theorem, we get $$\begin{align*} \mathbb{B}(f,g,h)&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\int_{\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G)\leq \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G)}\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G)d \mathbb{P}(\omega) + \int_{\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G) > \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G)}\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G)d \mathbb{P}(\omega)\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\int_{\Omega}\text{min}(\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G), \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G))d \mathbb{P}(\omega)\\&= \frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}[\text{min}(\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G), \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G))] \end{align*}$$ Now suppose we have another decoder $h’$ s.t. $h_i \neq h_i’$ on measureable $S \subset M$ for some $i \in [n]$. Observe that on $E$, every decoder performs with the same BER (i.e. $\frac{1}{2}\mathbb{P}(E)$) and modification of $h_i$ on any $G_{}(\mathbb{P})$ null set does not change the above expectation. Thus, for the sake of uniqueness, we only consider $h’$ s.t. $S \backslash E$ is not $G_{}(\mathbb{P})$ null, so WLOG redefine $S := S \backslash E$. Then $$\begin{align*} &\int_{\Omega}\mathbb{P}(U_{i}\neq h’{i}(G)|G)d \mathbb{P}(\omega)\\&= \int{S^{C}}\text{min}(\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G), \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G))d \mathbb{P}(\omega) + \int_{S}\text{max}(\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G), \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G))d \mathbb{P}(\omega) \end{align*}$$ By definition of $S$ we know that $$\text{max}(\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G), \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G)) > \text{min}(\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G), \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G))$$ Since $S$ is not null and because probabilities are non-negative, we must have $$\int_{S}\text{max}(\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G), \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G))d \mathbb{P}(\omega) > \int_{S}\text{min}(\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G), \mathbb{P}(U_{i}=0|G))d \mathbb{P}(\omega)$$ and therefore $$\int_{\Omega}\mathbb{P}(U_{i}\neq h’{i}(G)|G)d \mathbb{P}(\omega) > \int{\Omega}\mathbb{P}(U_{i}\neq h_{i}(G)|G)d \mathbb{P}(\omega)$$ Since this holds for some $i \in [n]$ and nonstrict inequalities hold for the others, we have that $$\mathbb{B}(f,g,h) < \mathbb{B}(f,g,h’)$$ $\blacksquare$

    Let $q: M \to [0, 1]$ be a measureable function and denote it our soft-decoding function. Define $Q = q \circ G$.

    Definition 3: The Binary Cross-Entropy of $(f,g,q)$ is: $$\begin{align*} \mathbb{C}(f,g,q) &= \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}-U_{i}\text{lg}Q_{i} - (1 - U_{i})\text{lg}(1-Q_{i})\right]\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}[-U_{i}\text{lg}Q_{i} - (1 - U_{i})\text{lg}(1-Q_{i})]\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[-U_{i}\text{lg}(q_{i}(G)) - (1 - U_{i})\text{lg}(1-q_{i}(G))|G]]\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}[-\mathbb{E}[1[U_{i}=1]\text{lg}(q_{i}(G))|G] - \mathbb{E}[1[U_{i}=0]\text{lg}(1-q_{i}(G))|G]] \end{align*}$$ Since $\text{lg}(q_i(G))$ and $\text{lg}(1 - q_i(G))$ are both $\sigma(G)$-measureable, we have that $$\begin{align*} \mathbb{C}(f,g,q) &=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}[-\text{lg}(q_{i}(G))\mathbb{E}[1[U_{i}=1]|G] - \text{lg}(1-q_{i}(G))\mathbb{E}[1[U_{i}=0]|G]]&\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}[-\text{lg}(q_{i}(G))\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G] - \text{lg}(1-q_{i}(G))\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=0|G]]\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}\Big[-\text{lg}(\frac{q_{i}(G)}{\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G]}\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G])\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G] - \text{lg}(\frac{1-q_{i}(G)}{\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=0|G]}\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=0|G])\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=0|G]\Big]\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}\Big[\Big( -\text{lg}\left( \frac{q_{i}(G)}{\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G]} \right) - \text{lg}(\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G]) \Big)\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G] \\&+ \Big( -\text{lg}\left( \frac{1-q_{i}(G)}{\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=0|G]} \right) - \text{lg}(\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=0|G]) \Big)\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=0|G]\Big]\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}\Big[-\text{lg}\left( \frac{q_{i}(G)}{\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G]} \right)\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G] -\text{lg}\left( \frac{1-q_{i}(G)}{\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=0|G]} \right)\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=0|G]\\&-\text{lg}(\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G])\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G]-\text{lg}(\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=0|G])\mathbb{P}[U_{i}=0|G]\Big]\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}[D_{KL}(\mathbb{P}[U_{i}|G] || q_{i}(G)) + \mathbb{H}{2}(\mathbb{P}[U{i}|G])]\\&=\frac{1}{n}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}[D_{KL}(\mathbb{P}[U_{i}|G] || q_{i}(G))] + \mathbb{H}(U_{i}|G) \end{align*}$$

    where $D_{KL}$ is the Kullbackâ€“Leibler Divergence, $\mathbb{H}2$ is the Binary Entropy function and $\mathbb{H}(X|Y)$ is the Conditional Entropy of random element $X$ given $Y$. Thus, our simplified definition of Binary Cross-Entropy is $$ \mathbb{C}(f,g,q) = \frac{1}{n}\sum\limits{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}[D_{KL}(\mathbb{P}[U_{i}|G] || q_{i}(G))] + \mathbb{H}(U_{i}|G) $$ Theorem 4: For a given choice of $f,g$, $\mathbb{C}{f,g}(q)$ has the unique minimizer ($G{*}(\mathbb{P})$-a.e.) $q$ defined component-wise as $$ q_i= \mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G] $$ Proof: This follows from Gibb’s Inequality, which states for random elements $A$, $B$,

    $$D_{KL}(A || B) \geq 0$$ with equality iff $A = B$ almost everywhere. Thus $\forall i \in [n]$ $$\mathbb{E}[D_{KL}(\mathbb{P}[U_{i}|G] || q_{i}(G))] = 0$$ iff $q_{i}(G) = \mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G]$ $G_{}(\mathbb{P})$-a.e.. Since $\mathbb{H}(U_i|G)$ is not a function of $q(G)$, we know $q_{i}(G) = \mathbb{P}[U_{i}=1|G]$ $G_{}(\mathbb{P})$-a.e. $\forall i \in [n]$ is the unique minimizer of $\mathbb{C}_{f,g}(q)$. $\blacksquare$

    This theorem tells us two things

    1. For some channel, given an encoder, the optimal decoder (for crossentropy) is the one that exactly computes the posterior probabilities for each bit. This naturally gives us the Bayes’ minimizer and thus the unique BER minimizer. On the other hand, it is possible that some soft decoders may give us the Bayes’ minimizer but do not minimize cross-entropy. So minimizing cross-entropy is stronger than simply minimizing BER (from the decoder’s perspective).
    2. Since $f,g$ determine the minimizing $h$, understanding the BCE minimizing code reduces to understanding the encoder that minimizes $\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n} \mathbb{H}(U_{i}|G)$

    Remark 5: Recall that the Mutual Information between $U_{i}$ and $G$ $\forall i \in [n]$ can be written as $$I(U_{i};G)= \mathbb{H}(U_{i}) - \mathbb{H}(U_{i}|G)$$ Since $\mathbb{H}(U_{i})$ is constant wrt $f,g,h$, minimizing BCE is equivalent to maximizing $$\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}I(U_{i};G)$$ Mutual Information is a measure of shared information between $U_{i}$ and $G$ (aka their mutual dependence). So minimizing BCE is, in effect, maximizing the dependence between $G$ and $U_{i}$. Since we are maximizing a sum, it is unclear whether there are any tradeoffs between the components.

    From here on out, we denote $\mathbb{B}(f,g)$ to be the bit error rate when using the Bayes Classifier decoder and $\mathbb{C}(f,g)$ to be the cross entropy when using the posterior on $U_i$ given $G$ as the soft-decoder for each $i \in [n]$. When we subscript either with $i \in [n]$, we are referring to that quantity only for bit $U_{i}$.

    Next we show some inequalities relating Conditional Entropy with Bit Error Rate.

    Theorem 6: The following inequalities hold for all choices of $f, g$:

    $$2\mathbb{B}{i}(f,g) \leq \mathbb{C}{i}(f,g) \leq \mathbb{H}{2}(\mathbb{B}{i}(f,g))$$ In particular $$2\mathbb{B}(f,g) \leq \mathbb{C}(f,g) \leq \frac{1}{n} \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n} \mathbb{H}{2}(\mathbb{B}{i}(f,g))$$

    Proof: Let $i \in [n]$ and fix $f,g$. Let $t = \min(\mathbb{P}(U_{i}=1|G), \mathbb{P}(U_i=0|G))$. Then $$\begin{align*} &\mathbb{B}{i}(f,g) = \mathbb{E}[t]\\&\mathbb{C}{i}(f,g) = \mathbb{H}(U_{i}|G) = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{H}2(t)] \end{align*}$$ Recall that Shannon Entropy is a Concave Function, so all line segments connecting two points of $\mathbb{H}2$ in $\left[0,\frac{1}{2}\right]$ lie in the undergraph of $\mathbb{H}2$. Since $\mathbb{H}{2}(0)= 0$ and $\mathbb{H}{2}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = 1$, we know that $\mathbb{H}{2}(s) \geq 2s$ $\forall s \in \left[0, \frac{1}{2}\right]$. Thus, since Expectation is Non-Decreasing, we have that

    $$\mathbb{C}{i}(f,g) = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{H}{2}(t)]\geq 2\mathbb{E}[t] = 2\mathbb{B}_{i}(f,g)$$ proving the LHS inequality.

    For the second, note again that Shannon Entropy is a Concave Function, so we can apply Jensen’s Inequality. Then

    $$\mathbb{C}{i}(f,g) = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{H}{2}(t)]\leq \mathbb{H}{2}(\mathbb{E}[t]) = \mathbb{H}{2}(\mathbb{B}_{i}(f,g))$$ giving us the RHS inequality.

    The second inequality follows by averaging together the inequalities for each component. $\blacksquare$

    Remarks 7:

    1. The application of Jensen’s Inequality in this proof gives us a special case of Fano’s Inequality when the symbol space is just 2 elements ($U_{i}$ is a binary random variable).
    2. Though this inequality bounds the Binary Cross-Entropy and relates it to the Bit Error Rate, it does not prove that a choice of $f$ that minimizes the Binary Cross-Entropy also minimizes the Bit Error Rate. I haven’t been able to find a counter-example that would show the minimizers can be different, and I think there might be a tighter relationship to be found between the two. It might be helpful to play around with specific choices of $g$ and $f$.
